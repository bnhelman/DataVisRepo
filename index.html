Data Cleaning Summary

Only the most important points are listed here for brevity and readability. Full details and justifications for the data manipulations are in Appendix A. Individual sections have their own cleanings if necessary.

  1. Prices below $10,000 were filtered out as outlier or family transactions
  2. Excluded properties with latitudes and longitudes outside of Boston (See Appendix A and the note on spatial transformations below)
  3. Verified the existence of $100M+ purchases as legitimate by address
  4. Noted that many sales included a buyer with 0 owned properties. Determined these were likely legitimate purchases from unknown/untracked buyers and not first time buyers (see Appendix A). Left these in, but removed them in 1 notable visualization

Advanced Data Transformations

  1. Latitude and Longitude Recovery: There were ~450 entries where the latitude and longitudes were entered as (0, 0). I determined that these were likely legitimate purchases by checking their address against their zip, then seeing if the price was reasonable for a sample. I noticed that an entry having a (0, 0) was correlated with their price and zip code. Removing them would have introduced a statistical bias because of this correlation and the number of entries. Therefore, I generated corrected latitudes and longitudes for these properties using their address as a reference and updated their entries.
  2. Inflation Adjustment: The prices were at time of sale, which means that if I want to fairly compare (real) prices across different years, then I must adjust for inflation. I retrieved the CPI-U for our dates using a base year of 2000 dollars. Then I adjusted every entry using the CPI for that entry's year and month. I didn't interpolate for the day. I did not adjust the profits from each sale or the annularized values, but I also did not use this data in my visualizations.

From my personal experiences, investors care about how assets appreciate and how this compares to inflation. So let's investigate house prices in Boston through the lens of appreciating assets instead of places to live.

The idea of a house as an appreciating asset rather than a place to live is a key idea for investors. This is of course why housing investment is popular in the first place. Not only does the asset appreciate, it also pays dividends in rent collection.
