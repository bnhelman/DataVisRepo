Critique

The graphic does a few things well, in particular, it is well targeted to the audience, especially given the context of the time. The first impression the graphic makes is to get an emotional reaction by analogy. The common viewer is likely to see this data as “spiraling out of control” because it uses a literal spiral, and the viewer is likely to understand this point at a glance even if the details are a bit messy. From reading the rest of the article, I think the designers intentionally try to make this apparent. An important plus for this “at a glance” impression is that it’s different from just a line chart. At the time, we were inundated with tons and tons of line charts, and in my personal experience, many people stopped looking at them. By having some novel representation, I assume people would be more likely to stop and look at it instead of skimming. Therefore, it’s well targeted for the intended audience. By wrapping the timeline, they’re able to fit more information into the same width than a linear timeline. One effect of this is that the graphic does a decent job of not fearmongering. By placing this spike in context with past ones, we get a frame of reference from our previous experiences. This aligns with our history, “we’ve had a few spikes before” while still guiding the inference, “but it looks like this one is going to be pretty bad.” I think it encourages informed caution without panic. Mechanically, I appreciated the location and sizing of the “7-day average” label. Most people wouldn’t care about this, and it stays out of the way for them. But for those who do care, they would look for it and then it would show up. This happened to me, where I didn’t care then right at the time when I thought about how they calculated this, I noticed the 7 day average. Unfortunately, the dates are the same size as this out of the way label.

The main issue I have with the design is that it’s not immediately obvious where I should start reading. Common pop culture typically depicts “spiraling in” in the sense of a “downward spiral” for which a common first look would try to read this by going outside in. I think the arrow does not do enough to show the direction. That being said, I understand the tradeoff of having the larger areas towards the outside. Unfortunately, It took me too long to get the direction and see the months delineating the seasons. It’s clever, but I didn’t immediately recognize the year cycle, which is a very important part of the design. I think this is a significant failure and I would be willing to trade some things in to recover this. Also many people have different mental images of how the years wrap. In my mind I actually see January on the x axis with the seasons going clockwise. This is simply because this is how it was shown to me in preschool. I appreciate that I can see the whole history of the pandemic, but it seems like the article is actually only focused on January 2022. That takes up very little of the visual space. Without reading the article, I would not have guessed that this visualization was actually trying to make a prediction rather than analyze past trends. I had no idea they were talking about Omicron. However, in the context of the time, most people would probably already be aware of it. While they gain a good “at a glance” effect, they trade in ease of comparing the scale of the graph, which I find hard to understand. Yes, they have a legend, but I see this at the end after I’ve already looked at the graph for a while. It’s unclear whether the width of the legend is referring to the biggest point being 150k or that specific width being 150k. This scales the data in different ways, so I think this is a more severe failing. I would put the scalebars into the diagram itself. Even among relative values, it is still difficult to compare because I must mentally translate and rotate widths through space. For example, does February 2021 or October 2021 have more cases? It’s difficult to tell.

Final Visualization

Final Writeup

The main sense of identity I wanted to preserve when altering this design was that it would be a good intro graphic on a New York Times article. This means leaning towards minimalism, trying to keep annotations few, authoritative phrasing, and being eye-catching. I wanted people to stop scrolling to read my pretend article. I further contextualized my analysis towards catering to viewers living in 2022. The spiral is very well fit for this because it serves three purposes: to be novel, to represent a calendar over multiple years, and to pack in data more densely than a linear timeline. As I mentioned in my critique section, people were overwhelmed by graphics at the time, meaning an interesting graphic like the spiral stood out among the crowd, likely making people actually read it. Whereas the spiral before was an emotional worry, because we have more data, here it’s more like the spiral eyes emoji, maybe conveying a tiredness of sorts. In my second sketch, I did try to make a broken linear timeline, but it was simply too difficult to fit all the data into it, and I lost most of the sense of seasons. I added annotations for the Delta and Omicron outbreaks because I felt that this would allow people to better place each part of the graph in context with their own experiences. I made a huge black center dot to give a starting point and I removed the center crossing of the grid so that it didn’t compete for attention in the center. I felt that the areal encoding of the case number along with the darker lines in the original design was compelling and good for snapshotting a lot of data quickly, so I went with their choices. I also went with their 7-day average because it smoothed the data out and kept the attention on trends rather than noise when I tested removing it. Unfortunately, this graphic does not do a good job of comparing values directly because of the need to rotate the data through space for comparison. This was a price I paid to have novelty. The beginning area of covid is downplayed in particular because of the radius scaling of area in polar coordinates. This makes the beginning seem less important and more obscured. Although with recency biases, maybe it isn’t so bad to emphasize the most recent data.

From my sketches and critiques I targeted 3 criteria I specifically wanted to improve that I felt was worth moving away from minimalism for. In order: to know where to start reading and how to continue, know that it’s a calendar, and know what scale thickness corresponds to what number of new daily cases. The first sketch was foundational to all of these, in which I designed a single natural track for the eyes to follow using arrows. In this final graphic, I have placed arrows along the path to both give the direction of the spiral and to hopefully constrain the viewer’s eyes. There are 3 exceptional points that are likely starting points for reading the graphic: the title, the center, and the omicron outbreak. To avoid introducing other locations, I’ve lightened the texts for 7-day average (inspired by the first sketch) and the September and August months. I’ve also left white space on all the corners except for the title corner to reduce stray entry directions into the center. The most desirable reading is first the title, from which naturally follows the scale (out of context for now), then white space, at which it’s likely the eyes are brought to another of the 2 exceptional points. Hopefully, the eyes pass over the 200k scale annotation before reaching the middle, this was intentional. Once in the middle, if the viewer did not notice the outer calendar, they will likely still see the 3 months Feb, March, April…, and they will still get the understanding of a calendar. They then next see up to 3 scattered scale bars to understand the area and give time footholds, hitting the omicron section. If they started in the omicron section, then by default they should at least know the scale. I include the August and September months as a backup for “Omicron-starters” to understand the calendar reasonably quickly. Since the months don’t continue past October, I hope that the eyes are shuttled either to the title or to the center. The three different starting points all give the viewer answers to the 3 criteria I specifically wanted to fix within a tolerable amount of time. There was a major trade off between using a novel graphic and clarity of information. It aligned better with my make-believe scenario of wanting viewers to lean into the novel graphic. In defense of the spiral graphic, a perfectly clear line chart that no one reads conveys ironically no information at all. Did you spend the time to read the line chart they provided later in the original article? In the end I was not able to find a way to have both the spiral and also convey magnitudes across points, which was part of my original critique. I tried adding a color-code like in sketch 3 (see Appendix A), which works great if you’ve already seen the original graph. However, if you don’t know how to read the graph already, there’s too much information clutter and it ends up defeating the point of a great “first glance.” The process of critique by design definitely helped by giving specific goals to work towards instead of vaguely trying to make it better. By listing my priorities and values out, I had a framework to make decisions later which felt more cohesive.
