Critique

The graphic does a few things well, in particular, it is well targeted to the audience, especially given the context of the time. The first impression the graphic makes is to get an emotional reaction by analogy. The common viewer is likely to see this data as “spiraling out of control” because it uses a literal spiral, and the viewer is likely to understand this point at a glance even if the details are a bit messy. From reading the rest of the article, I think the designers intentionally try to make this apparent. An important plus for this “at a glance” impression is that it’s different from just a line chart. At the time, we were inundated with tons and tons of line charts, and in my personal experience, many people stopped looking at them. By having some novel representation, I assume people would be more likely to stop and look at it instead of skimming. Therefore, it’s well targeted for the intended audience. By wrapping the timeline, they’re able to fit more information into the same width than a linear timeline. One effect of this is that the graphic does a decent job of not fearmongering. By placing this spike in context with past ones, we get a frame of reference from our previous experiences. This aligns with our history, “we’ve had a few spikes before” while still guiding the inference, “but it looks like this one is going to be pretty bad.” I think it encourages informed caution without panic. Mechanically, I appreciated the location and sizing of the “7-day average” label. Most people wouldn’t care about this, and it stays out of the way for them. But for those who do care, they would look for it and then it would show up. This happened to me, where I didn’t care then right at the time when I thought about how they calculated this, I noticed the 7 day average. Unfortunately, the dates are the same size as this out of the way label.

The main issue I have with the design is that it’s not immediately obvious where I should start reading. Common pop culture typically depicts “spiraling in” in the sense of a “downward spiral” for which a common first look would try to read this by going outside in. I think the arrow does not do enough to show the direction. That being said, I understand the tradeoff of having the larger areas towards the outside. Unfortunately, It took me too long to get the direction and see the months delineating the seasons. It’s clever, but I didn’t immediately recognize the year cycle, which is a very important part of the design. I think this is a significant failure and I would be willing to trade some things in to recover this. Also many people have different mental images of how the years wrap. In my mind I actually see January on the x axis with the seasons going clockwise. This is simply because this is how it was shown to me in preschool. I appreciate that I can see the whole history of the pandemic, but it seems like the article is actually only focused on January 2022. That takes up very little of the visual space. Without reading the article, I would not have guessed that this visualization was actually trying to make a prediction rather than analyze past trends. I had no idea they were talking about Omicron. However, in the context of the time, most people would probably already be aware of it. While they gain a good “at a glance” effect, they trade in ease of comparing the scale of the graph, which I find hard to understand. Yes, they have a legend, but I see this at the end after I’ve already looked at the graph for a while. It’s unclear whether the width of the legend is referring to the biggest point being 150k or that specific width being 150k. This scales the data in different ways, so I think this is a more severe failing. I would put the scalebars into the diagram itself. Even among relative values, it is still difficult to compare because I must mentally translate and rotate widths through space. For example, does February 2021 or October 2021 have more cases? It’s difficult to tell.

Data Cleaning Summary

Only the most important points are listed here for brevity and readability. Full details and justifications for the data manipulations are in Appendix A. Individual sections have their own cleanings if necessary.

  1. Prices below $10,000 were filtered out as outlier or family transactions
  2. Excluded properties with latitudes and longitudes outside of Boston (See Appendix A and the note on spatial transformations below)
  3. Verified the existence of $100M+ purchases as legitimate by address
  4. Noted that many sales included a buyer with 0 owned properties. Determined these were likely legitimate purchases from unknown/untracked buyers and not first time buyers (see Appendix A). Left these in, but removed them in 1 notable visualization

Advanced Data Transformations

  1. Latitude and Longitude Recovery: There were ~450 entries where the latitude and longitudes were entered as (0, 0). I determined that these were likely legitimate purchases by checking their address against their zip, then seeing if the price was reasonable for a sample. I noticed that an entry having a (0, 0) was correlated with their price and zip code. Removing them would have introduced a statistical bias because of this correlation and the number of entries. Therefore, I generated corrected latitudes and longitudes for these properties using their address as a reference and updated their entries.
  2. Inflation Adjustment: The prices were at time of sale, which means that if I want to fairly compare (real) prices across different years, then I must adjust for inflation. I retrieved the CPI-U for our dates using a base year of 2000 dollars. Then I adjusted every entry using the CPI for that entry's year and month. I didn't interpolate for the day. I did not adjust the profits from each sale or the annularized values, but I also did not use this data in my visualizations.

Investor Flows

The above graphs reveals a trend that I would not have expected. In general, investors "stay in their lane," trading only with firms that are of similar size to their own. This is an answer to one of the original questioned I asked "how does money flow differently between the different sized investors?" It would appear that there are in fact sub markets where investors are primarily buying from similar investors. Why might this be? One explanation could be that different sized firms achieve different economies of scale for managing buildings. This agrees with the reading where larger firms can extract a higher profit margin. To investigate this further we would need to break down the flows by purchase price.

I was also surprised to see that non-investors still dominate the number of properties exchanged, even if they are losing in the cash value of those sales. This is based both on the readings claiming that investors are buying tons of property and also because of the previous figure showing that investors are dominating in amount spent recently. This raises the same followup question of "What kinds of properties are the different kinds of investors buying?" One explanation could be that although intitutional investors are buying few properties, they're buying expensive ones such as large buildings. Then in the data this might only count as a single property, but actually houses many apartments. So to answer this question, we should further categorize these flows through channels identifying the price of the property being purchased.

Indeed, the largest investors are purchasing the most expensive properties, explaining the different indicators we found before. Therefore, even though they don't show up on paper as owning a huge number of houses, the largest investors may own a few multi million dollar apartment complexes that might only count as "one building," or serve some other purpose like a mall. This trend is extremely well resolved in the flow charts, even too well for normal data. Further thinking reveals that this is in retrospect unsurprising. One of the definitions we used for categorizing investor size was the amount the spent. So by definition investors buying expensive properties would be larger. So a medium firm that suddenly bought a $100,000,000 property would then be considered an institutional firm by definition, likely yielding this strong signal in the data.

But if we hold to our definitions, then yes, there does appear to be a submarket, and properties do indeed seem to flow within these channels.

Flipping

We know that flipping is becoming an increasingly common occurance in the housing market. But what kind of houses are being flipped? We know now that investors tend to stick in their channels, so in addition to the motivations given in the question statement, are homes that are flipped more likely to be continually reflipped? If there are some time correlation, we should be able to see this by looking at the conditional chances of a house getting flipped based on if it was flipped already. Note that this does not give us an idea about spatial correlations like whether a certain area is more likely to be flipped.

I've included two visualizations because they tell slightly different stories. When placed on top of each other, the bar graph highlights the difference to make it obvious that the chances are different. 1.6x and 1.3x are pretty obvious on a bar graph. However, we were interested in whether a submarket exists for these homes to be continually flipped. When put on pie charts, the percentages are still there but its much less profound because we lose the contrast of the data being stacked right next to each other. I also note the samples size on the pie chart, which was significantly decreasing due to the nature of taking a small percent of a small percent. So the pie chart provides some caution against only looking at contrast. In the previous sections, we saw a submarket (investor size) that was nearly completely trading within itself. Here the difference is small relative to the effect we saw there. There is no "cascade" of flipping that would result. So I come to the following conclusions:

  1. It is more likely for a flipped property to be flipped again
  2. Continuously flipped properties only constitute a weak submarket. This does not occur at a high enough rate (in only 9.27% of cases) for this to constitute an strong submarket.
One caveat to the above analysis is that we have defined flipping as a resale within 2 years. Because flipping is a more recent development, if a house was first flipped in 2020, it's unlikely that it would flip for a third time simply because the data does not continue past 2022. So the statistical bias from the recency of the flipping practice makes a time correlation more difficult to resolve. Even if it is actually there, it might not have had time to develope to where we could see it.

Influence of Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae

The data conveniently has a flag for whether the property was bought/sold by a Government Sponsored Entity (GSE). In their metadata, they say this simply means that the buyer/seller was Freddie Mac or Fannie Mae. So as a first step lets look at data with this signal.

Based on the propublica article, I was expecting that there would be a huge number of Freddie Mac and Freddie Mae properties. For example, the article says that Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae participated in many deals in recent years that were, "massive, involving thousands of apartments and multiple states" (propublica, https://www.propublica.org/article/when-private-equity-becomes-your-landlord). So where are all those houses? It's not just that there are a small number of purchases in recent years, there are absolutely 0 since 2015. The chances that Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae were purchasing thousands of houses and not one was in this dataset are astronomically low. Something is wrong here.

This line of reasoning led me to thinking about what exactly the data represents. I came to understand that the data is talking about direct purchase of the property. Since Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae are intended to buy mortgages in the secondary market, they shouldn't appear as buyers. In fact, they shouldn't appear at all. So now we have the opposite problem, why do we have 34 entries that are labeled as bought by Freddie Mac or Fannie Mae?

I looked into this further (google + Gemini) and came up with the following explanation. Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae almost never own properties directly. Instead they provide liquidity by purchasing loans on the secondary market. To oversimplify, if they end up with the purchase of a property, something has gone awry. The owner would have had to default on the mortgage and then the property would have had to have gone through foreclosure (or deed in leiu). Then, the property would have had to have still failed to be purchased at the foreclosure auction beyond the mortgage equity. Then Freddie Mac acquires the property through an REO (Real Estate Owned) transaction. This is an involuntary process, and is in general undesireable for Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae. (source: initially Gemini, then I fact checked against https://servicing-guide.fanniemae.com/svc/e-4.1-01/notifying-fannie-mae-acquired-property, https://www.fhfa.gov/news/testimony/an-examination-of-the-federal-housing-finance-agencys-real-estate-owned-reo-pilot-program). There are a lot of subtleties, but in general, homes that are owned by Freddie mac went through foreclosure and were undesireable enough that they were not purchased at foreclosure auction. So the data likely only represents houses who foreclosed AND did not get bought at the foreclosure auction.

That would explain the uptick following the recession where there were a lot of foreclosures. But it again raises the question of why are there no datapoints after 2015? I again looked into this with a combination of google and Gemini and discovered that Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae changed their policy for REO in 2015 to get rid of these properties when the loans were not performing (Lane, https://www.housingwire.com/articles/33450-fannie-mae-to-sell-non-performing-loans/). These are called non performing loans (NPLs) and are categorized when the loans are delinquent and are likely to default. The change in policy allowed Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae to sell their NPLs BEFORE the foreclosure process. This led to sell offs of huge numbers of NPLs before they were foreclosed on ( https://www.fanniemae.com/newsroom/fannie-mae-news/fannie-mae-announces-initial-sale-non-performing-loans, https://freddiemac.gcs-web.com/news-releases/news-release-details/freddie-mac-executes-largest-ever-sale-seriously-delinquent/) Therefore, since our signal is only houses that make it all the way past the point of a foreclosure auction, we lose our signal. Let's represent this as a timeline.

That seems to explain our data very well. If these were due to REOs, we would expect Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae to sell them off very quickly once they got on their books. In general, they're not supposed to be owning these properties directly, so they need to get rid of them. So if we track each address individually, we can locate when it was sold by Freddie Mac or Fannie Mae in time and also see the price. If they are sold very quickly, it's likely the REO hypothesis is correct, but if they're held for long amounts of time, then it's likely Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae were actually intending to hold the properties.

And yes, most properties are sold off quickly, aligning with the scenerio I had described before. It's likely that Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae are selling off the mortages for NPLs before they get foreclosed on, explaining our loss of signal in 2015.

So, if Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae began selling off their NPLs in 2015, who is buying them? This question cannot be answered with the dataset by definition (we lose the signal, afterall), but I looked into this and found agreement with the propublica article. A huge number of these are being bought up by large institutions like Goldman Sachs (https://www.mpamag.com/us/specialty/commercial/goldman-sachs-fannie-maes-biggest-buyer-of-non-performing-loans/63005) and PE firms (https://freddiemac.gcs-web.com/news-releases/news-release-details/freddie-mac-sells-11-billion-seriously-delinquent-loans/). The article also explains that these NPLs are being sold off in bulk packages in massive deals, effectively making it so that only large investors can buy them. This practice excludes entities with less bankroll like non-investors from participating. So, why are large PE investors snapping up thousands of loans which were likely headed to foreclosure? With the explosion of flipping, it's likely they are looking to get in cheap, and resell the house after renovating it.

Summary

I was able to determine that actually yes, there appears to be clear submarkets that investors channel their cash through broken down by size of the investor. We also noted that invested properties do not really mix between different sized investors. Although much of this can be attributed to how we have defined the size of the investor through their total value of properties.

It appears that once a home is flipped, it is 1.6x more likely to get flipped again, and then 1.3x more likely to be flipped "again again." However, the absolute percentage chances are still small, only 9.3% and 7.8% as compared to the normal 5.9%. While flipping does have some kind of time correlation, it is not strong enough to establish the same kind of submarket we observed based on investor size and property price. So there exist a weak submarket where homes are reflipped but getting flipped once does not cause cascades of flippings.

I was not able to directly answer my question about how Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae are contributing to wealth contraction in Boston because I couldn't find enough data in the dataset. However, I did uncover an oddity in the data which led me to an interesting explanation involving the selloff of delinquent loans to large firms. I believe this practice definitionally fits the bill of increasing wealth inequality because they are transfering assets from people who are struggling to make ends meet to the ultra wealthy.
